TITLE: on-campus vs. off campus?
---
[POST]
An incoming Rutgers freshman asks whether to live on campus or off campus. They want a quiet personal space for studying and relaxing but also want to socialize. They prefer being alone for housing but are open to advice. They also have a car and ask whether that changes the decision.

[REPLY 1]
A respondent strongly recommends living on campus for the first year. Their main argument is that dorm life helps with social adjustment, meeting people, and building a friend group. They acknowledge that roommates can be hard but say students can move off campus after freshman year. They also note that students can use libraries and study lounges if the dorm room is not ideal for studying.

[REPLY 2]
Another respondent argues that on-campus living can be noisy and social, which may hurt studying for students who need quiet. They say students can still make friends while living off campus but warn that off-campus housing can be more expensive.

[REPLY 3]
A respondent disagrees that on-campus living automatically hurts grades. They say living on campus can help academically and socially because students are closer to classes, classmates, professors, study groups, and office hours. They also say dorms are not always loud all the time and that many students study in lounges or libraries.

[REPLY 4]
The same respondent says the cost of off-campus living varies greatly by rental. Their own off-campus lease was cheaper than a dorm for the academic year, but they caution that costs depend on rent, food, and other expenses.

[REPLY 5]
The same respondent explains that campus parking is handled by permit. A resident permit allows parking in certain lots on the assigned campus, and the distance from the dorm depends on the campus.

[REPLY 6]
Another respondent says first-year students should generally live on campus because it helps them meet people and adjust to Rutgers. They see benefits on both sides for non-first-year students. Off campus can be cheaper financially, but students must handle transportation and apartment or house issues themselves. On campus may be easier for maintenance and support.

[REPLY 7]
Another respondent tells the student to actually look at off-campus options and costs before deciding. They emphasize being ready to decide quickly if a good deal appears. They mention Facebook housing pages as a search tool.

[REPLY 8]
A respondent is conflicted about freshman on-campus housing, saying it can be good or bad depending on roommate compatibility. They add that on-campus housing may feel safer than some unaffiliated off-campus housing and cite a crime alert example from 2013 as a reason students should consider safety carefully.

[REPLY 9]
Another reply says if the student lives far away and cannot inspect off-campus housing in person, taking a dorm assignment first may be safer until they understand the area. They suggest waiting before moving off campus so the student does not lose already-paid housing money.

[KEY TAKEAWAYS]
- On-campus housing can help first-year students adjust socially and academically.
- Off-campus housing can provide privacy and possibly lower cost, but tradeoffs include leases, food costs, safety checks, transportation, maintenance, and less built-in social life.
- Having a car helps but does not solve every problem, since Rutgers resident parking is permit-based and off-campus parking varies by rental.
- Advice from this thread is older and should be treated as historical perspective, not current Rutgers policy.
